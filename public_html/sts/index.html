By Jackson Dawkins


Skepticism and cynicism are often confused.

These two will be our illustrations for how skeptics and cynics look in different scenarios.
Let's begin



Scenario 1

Steve and Cindy both meet people who challenge their views on politics (this never happens in real life).



Steve is open to the ideas that the person proposes. Steve has ideas of his own, however, so he would require evidence to consider changing his opinion.


Cindy is a firm believer that she is right and thinks that no amount of evidence could change that.


Scenario 2

Steve and Cindy are working in a group, and the group is faced with a difficult problem to solve.
The group proposes an idea that Steve and Cindy both disagree with.



Steve considers the problem and the proposed solution. He thinks of a better solution, and proposes it instead.


Cindy is focused on the fact that the solution is wrong. Rather than proposing a new solution, she simply shuts down the others.


Scenario 3

Someone in Steve and Cindy's group skipped the team's meeting,
causing them to both stay up all night to finish the project.



Steve is angry at the person, but realizes that he may not know the whole situation. He thinks the best of people first, assuming that the person did not mean to offload so much work onto the rest of the team.


Cindy is also angry at the person, but chooses to blame the person's character. She assumes the worst of people first, not willing to give the person's situation any thought.


At The End of The Day...



Steve has had a hard day, but is still hopeful and accepting of people.


Cindy has had a hard day too, and is done dealing with people entirely. She wouldn't even pose for an appropriate picture.
